### Stimulus: Seawall Library: A Community Lifeline

Seawall Library began as a small collection of donated books in a coastal community hall. Over the past five years it has expanded into a volunteer-run space offering free access to books, internet, and weekly workshops for all ages. During these events, the library has become an information hub where residents gather to receive updates, recharge devices, and attend emergency-preparedness talks.

The library’s volunteer team includes retired teachers, university students, and fishermen who help with outreach. Funding comes from small grants, local business donations, and a monthly community fundraising market. The market sells crafts, second-hand books, and homemade foods; profits cover utilities and new materials. Volunteers report that community ownership—people recognising the library as belonging to everyone—is the project’s most valuable asset.

Organisers also track usage statistics to plan services. Last year they recorded 4,200 library visits, 600 laptop sessions, and 120 participants in workshops. Younger children attend story hours while teenagers often use the internet for school research. Some adults participate in literacy classes and small business advice sessions. The team hopes to expand hours and add mobile outreach to nearby fishing settlements, but they need steady funding and more volunteers to sustain growth.

The library’s leadership is careful to include diverse voices in planning. Meetings are scheduled at different times to accommodate shift-workers, and notices are posted in English and in the community creole. Organisers welcome suggestions and invite residents to monthly ‘idea forums’ where improvements are discussed openly. They believe the library, by offering both practical resources and social space, contributes to long-term community well-being.

### Stimulus: The Gardener's Chair

Marla found the chair under the crabapple tree the day after the storm. It had been a plain wooden chair once—its back slats smoothed by hands long gone, its legs slightly splayed from years of someone leaning back on a summer afternoon. Now the paint flaked in pale green curls and a single gardenia petal lay asleep on the seat.

She hesitated before she lifted it, thinking of the neighbor who had tended this yard for decades and who, this spring, had moved to a small apartment with a view of the bus stop and the laundromat. The house opposite was quieter now; its curtains rarely opened. Marla remembered the old man in a floppy hat with his slow, deliberate movements as he coaxed tomatoes from the earth. He had hummed to the seedlings as if they were children learning to walk.

Taking the chair into her own yard felt like accepting a story. Marla set it beneath the plum tree where the late afternoon light pooled in dappled coins. She sat and felt the give of old wood cupping her weight; it fit to the curve of her back as if it had been waiting. She closed her eyes and let the silence of the street — punctuated by the distant rumble of a bus and the occasional bark — fold around her.

She began to come here daily. Sometimes she brought a book she never opened, other times a mug of coffee that went lukewarm as she watched a lone sparrow hop along the fence. The chair collected small things: a ribbon caught in the armrest, a smear of soil where a seed pod had burst, the faint spiral of a snail's trail. Neighbors started to notice. A little boy placed a toy truck near the chair’s foot. A woman waved when she passed with her grocery bag, and once, a teenager lingered long enough to ask if the seat was taken.

That was when Marla learned the chair had a history. The teenager, whose hair smelled of the salon down the block, told a small tale about how the old gardener had carved a tiny heart into the underside of the seat, a secret token, he said, for anyone who’d ever loved a garden. Marla knelt and ran her finger along the grain; there, worn smooth by time and palms, was the shallow heart, nearly invisible.

She thought of how objects hold quiet witness: how the chair had seen the gardener plant seeds and mourn his wife's brief illness, how it had cradled anniversaries and small reconciliations, how its legs had dug into the soil on a windy day when a stray cat sought shelter beneath. The chair seemed to keep each memory like a layer of varnish — not exactly visible, but felt in the way it softened the edges of a hard day.

Autumn came and the garden went gold. The chair stayed under the plum tree, now a stage for falling leaves and the occasional snowflake. One evening, as Marla sat with her scarf pulled tight and the city lights pricking awake, she understood something simple: that by sitting in that chair, by accepting its history, she had become part of the same quiet story. The chair had offered her a small inheritance — not of money or land, but a continuity, a place where days could be stitched together and made to mean more than a hurry.

### Stimulus: Editorial: A Town That Plants Its Future

When the council proposed cutting funding for community green spaces, I thought of the old mango grove at the foot of Cedar Hill. It was where children learned how seasons change, where elders greeted the day with familiar paths, and where, in a storm, the whole neighbourhood found shelter. Green spaces are not luxuries; they are the quiet infrastructure of a healthy town. They stabilise soil, temper heat islands, improve air quality and support pollinators that keep our gardens productive.

Cutting their funding saves pennies today but costs us dearly in health, biodiversity and community connection. Instead of withdrawing support, the council should adopt a planting plan that engages local schools and businesses. A modest annual budget can leverage volunteers and corporate sponsorship to maintain existing parks and create pocket gardens in empty lots. With planning, maintenance and community education, the benefits multiply: fewer heat-related illnesses, more opportunities for recreation, and stronger bonds among neighbours.

I urge council members to think long-term. Vote to protect and expand our green infrastructure; it is an investment in the town’s resilience and in the everyday wellbeing of all residents.
