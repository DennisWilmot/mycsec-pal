## Question 1 - Module 1

### Community Garden: Growing Food and Connections

The morning sun warmed the raised beds as neighbours arrived with gloves and laughter. The community garden had started as a small pilot project two years earlier, an idea born at a parish meeting to address food access and create shared space. Now, it occupied the unused strip beside the primary school and produced vegetables, herbs and fruit that supplemented many households' meals. Volunteers followed a simple rotation of tasks: watering, weeding, mulching and harvesting, and newcomers were paired with experienced gardeners who taught planting techniques and composting.

Aside from food, the garden offered practical lessons. Schoolchildren visited weekly to observe plant life cycles and measure growth, connecting classroom lessons to the soil. Young people learning construction skills helped build benches and raised beds, supervised by a retired carpenter. Members kept records of planting dates, yields and weather patterns to plan the next season. These logs revealed that staggered planting and intercropping reduced pest problems and lengthened harvest periods.

Funding came from small grants, bake sales and donations from local businesses. The group maintained a modest budget for seeds, tools and irrigation repairs. When heavy rains damaged the shed, volunteers organised a weekend repair and raised the funds needed, reinforcing community resilience. A share system distributed surplus produce: families in need received weekly boxes, while contributors took home what they had grown.

The garden also became a meeting place. Informal conversations flourished among age groups, and cultural exchange occurred as elders introduced traditional recipes and younger members experimented with new varieties. Monthly potlucks celebrated harvest milestones and created occasions to discuss other neighbourhood concerns. The organisers hoped the garden model might be replicated in other districts, seeing it as a low-cost, high-impact approach to improving nutrition, fostering skills and strengthening social ties.

**(b)** Using the extract, summarise in no more than 50 words how the community garden benefits the neighbourhood. Use three clear points. Use three points and no more than 50 words. **(7 marks)**

### The Night Ferry

The ferry cut a slow, steady path through the dark, the island's lights receding into a ribbon of orange behind it. On deck, the air smelled of salt and diesel; the breeze moved the loose pages of a newspaper someone had abandoned. Passengers clustered under the awning, wrapped in jackets despite the warmth of the day. A lone street lamp on the quay flickered as gulls circled, their cries intermittently swallowed by the engine's hum.

Marla leaned on the rail, hands chilled by the spray. Ahead, the sea opened into a flat, starless plain. She thought of the thin letter in her bag and the farewell she had practised in the mirror until it sounded like someone else's voice. Around her, families murmured, fishermen slept in a tangle of nets, and a boy pointed at phosphorescence lighting the ferry's wake like a line of lanterns. The vessel's lights cast strangers' faces into gold and shadow; a man with tired eyes hummed an old tune.

Inside, the corridor smelled of wet rubber and coffee. The cabin door bore stickers from other voyages, sun-faded and curling at the edges. Marla remembered nights on this route years ago, when the crossings were filled with laughter and songs, before the long quiet that followed a closure at the port. Now the ferry felt smaller, more intimate, as if events onboard drew people inward. She tightened the strap on her bag and rehearsed the first words she would say at morning's meeting.

Beyond the bow, the horizon remained a low, uncertain line. The motion of the sea was steady but indifferent. Marla thought of the land she was leaving and the unknown she sailed toward; that sense of being suspended between two worlds thinned the certainty out of her breath. The ferry's horn sounded, a slow, longing note that seemed to agree with all the goodbyes carried in passengers' pockets.

### Start Later, Learn Better: Why Caribbean Schools Should Shift High School Timetables

Across the Caribbean, high-school students are routinely expected to be alert and productive in classes that begin as early as 7:00 a.m. Yet biological research and local experience both show that most teenagers need more sleep and naturally fall asleep later than younger children or adults. By moving the official start of the school day to 8:30 a.m., schools would help students sleep longer, learn more effectively and stay safer on the roads.

First, more sleep improves concentration and memory. When teenagers get adequate rest, they are better able to focus on lessons, retain information and perform well on tests. Several regional schools that piloted later starts reported fewer inattentive students and improvements in test scores after the change.

Second, later starts reduce the risk of accidents. Early-morning travel—often in darkness on rural roads—has contributed to crashes involving young drivers and pedestrians. Starting school later pushes commutes into daylight, lowering accident rates and keeping students safer.

Third, better-rested students experience improved mental health. Chronic sleep deprivation increases irritability, anxiety and depression among adolescents. Schools that allow a later start create an environment where students are less stressed and more emotionally stable, supporting both learning and wellbeing.

Some argue that a later start would conflict with after-school sports, jobs and family obligations. This is a valid concern, but schools can adapt by slightly adjusting extracurricular schedules, shortening non-essential meetings and coordinating with community partners so that most activities still fit into students’ days.

Change requires leadership. School administrators, parents and students should form committees to review local timetables, consult health experts and run short-term trials. If these pilots show benefits, boards should adopt later start times across the district. The evidence is clear: shifting the school day later is a manageable step that will yield safer, healthier and more successful students. Act now—ask your school board to pilot a later start this term.

**(b)** Summarize the passage in your own words. Use three points and no more than 50 words. **(7 marks)**
